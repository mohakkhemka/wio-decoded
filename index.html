Two friction points worth noting
Cost transparency. QWE runs OTC via Fuze. Users pay 0.5% commission plus an embedded OTC spread — the effective all-in cost is not shown before order placement. Active crypto traders who have used other platforms will notice this.
AED denomination. Crypto is a globally USD-priced asset class. Many users think, size, and plan positions in USD. Placing orders in AED without a USD view option creates friction — particularly for users who track portfolio performance in USD terms.
No leverage or perps. Active crypto traders want perpetual futures and margin. Without it, QWE serves as a spot accumulation product — suitable for passive holders but limited for active traders.
Part 1 · Point 4
Key takeaways
Five data-driven observations ranked by commercial impact. Where the data leads, and what it suggests.
Takeaway 1 — Power traders are the platform
Revenue
1,619 power traders generate 67.5% of all volume
Fewer than 1,700 users drive two thirds of the platform's trading activity. These are not casual investors — they average 251 trades over 5 months, trade two-way, and operate across multiple products. Every major brokerage (Zerodha in India, Sarwa and Robinhood in their respective markets) derives the majority of revenue from exactly this segment. Wio's priority should be deepening the product for these users — not just acquiring more occasional traders. A $20/month Pro subscription for full Depth of Market (DOM) access would create non-trading recurring revenue (NTRR) from the segment most likely to pay for it.
Takeaway 2 — Pricing friction is suppressing activation in crypto and metals
Activation
54.6% of crypto accounts and ~46% of metals accounts have never traded
These users opened product sub-accounts — intent was expressed. Crypto (QWE) carries 0.5% commission plus an embedded OTC spread, with no pre-trade cost disclosure. Gold/Silver (ASD) has a $1 minimum fee — on a $100 position that is 1%, on a $200 position it is 0.5%. In an era of low-cost investing, these friction points are meaningful. Fixing pricing transparency and reviewing minimums is likely to improve activation without requiring any new user acquisition. Additionally, adding perpetuals and futures for both crypto and metals serves active traders who want leverage and hedging tools — and likely drives higher volume from the existing base.
Takeaway 3 — Fixing pricing unlocks the untapped activation base
Growth
Account gaps across crypto and metals represent in-platform growth with zero acquisition cost
13,068 QWE accounts and 8,123 ASD accounts were opened but never traded. These users are already onboarded, already KYC-verified, and already inside the app. Converting even 10–15% of these dormant accounts to active traders — through better pricing, transparency, and product depth — is a material volume and revenue opportunity with no additional marketing spend required.
Takeaway 4 — RTY concentration is a platform risk
Risk
83.6% of volume from a single asset class
RTY (US equities) accounts for $4.07B of $4.87B total volume. This is a single-product dependency — if US equity markets have a prolonged downturn, trading volumes fall materially. Historically, equity downturns drive rotation into gold, silver, and crypto — assets where Wio already has product infrastructure but underdeveloped volume. Strengthening crypto and metals offerings creates natural hedge against RTY concentration and captures rotation flows when they occur.
Takeaway 5 — The addressable market is 25–35, digital-native, and underserved on execution tools
Market
US stocks, crypto, and metals will do the heavy lifting — derivatives complete the picture
The buy-side dominance (70.4% buy across the platform) confirms accumulation behaviour — this is a demographic building wealth, not speculating recklessly. But within that, 4,595 daily traders already trade two-way and behave like derivatives users. Options (puts for protection, calls for leverage) and futures (directional bets, hedging) are the natural next product layer. Power traders specifically need execution infrastructure: real charts, order depth, DOM, and ladder trading. Without it, sophisticated strategies get routed elsewhere.
Data assumptions & limitations
  • Dataset covers Apr–Aug 2025 only (5 months). All conclusions are window-limited. Current user base is likely materially larger.
  • AED values normalised to USD at 3.67 throughout for cross-product comparisons.
  • Product codes anonymised — decoded by cross-referencing trade patterns and wioinvest.io product page.
  • Commission rates confirmed where verified in-app. ZXC exchange fees not confirmed.
  • BNM and IOP product identifications are best guesses — low confidence. Excluded from strategic recommendations.

02
Fix the cost structure — then expand the product
54.6% of QWE accounts never traded. ~46% of ASD accounts never traded. Pricing and transparency can be meaningfully improved.
Bet 2
The problems
Crypto — cost and denomination
Pricing opacity. QWE charges 0.5% commission plus an embedded OTC spread via Fuze. Users see no cost breakdown before placing an order. Active crypto traders who have used other platforms will calculate the all-in cost and compare unfavourably.
AED denomination friction. Crypto is a globally USD-priced asset class. Many users think, size, and plan positions in USD. Placing and tracking orders in AED without a USD view option creates unnecessary friction.
Metals — minimum fee friction
$1 minimum on ASD. A $100 position in gold costs $1 in commission — 1% before any market movement. A $200 position costs 0.5%. For a product targeting wealth accumulators with smaller ticket sizes, this is a meaningful barrier. The median ASD trade is $781, but small-ticket users are likely being priced out before they start.
The fix
Show real bid/ask before execution
Live bid, live ask, spread as explicit % — shown before order confirmation. Standard on every exchange.
USD toggle for crypto
Switch between AED and USD view — prices, order sizing, and P&L all reflect selected currency. Simple, high-impact UX change.
Review gold/silver minimum fee structure
$1 minimum on a $100 gold position = 1% cost before any market move. Reduce to lower barrier for small-ticket entry.
Add crypto perpetuals via Fuze — explore regulatory path
Explore whether Fuze's existing structure covers perps. Do not launch before regulatory path is confirmed.
Add metals perpetuals and futures
Serves hedgers (protecting equity positions) and speculators (leveraged directional bets). In equity downturns, traders rotate to metals — Wio should capture that flow.
Adding more crypto coins rarely drives sustainable growth — altcoin interest is cyclical and fades. BTC, ETH, and XRP cover the vast majority of retail demand. The opportunity is depth of offering — better pricing, better execution, perps — not a longer asset list.

What is an option?
An option gives the holder the right, not the obligation to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price (the strike) before a set date (expiry). You pay a premium upfront — that's your maximum risk.
CALL holder
Right to buy the underlying at the strike. Profits when price rises.
PUT holder
Right to sell the underlying at the strike. Profits when price falls.
Who uses options?
⚡
Traders
Directional bets with defined risk. Control 100 shares for a fraction of the cost.
🛡️
Hedgers
Buy puts to protect an existing portfolio without selling your position.
💰
Income strategies
Sell covered calls on stocks you hold. Generate regular premium income.

Part 2 · Point 3
The customer problem
What gap does Wio V2 fill — and why now? Three layers of problem, each distinct.
Problem 1 — The UAE trading market is built on CFD brokers
The dominant model is fundamentally conflicted
Hundreds of CFD brokers in UAE — ranging from well-regulated to barely regulated. Pricing set by a liquidity provider, not an exchange. No independent price discovery.
Broker is the counterparty — when you lose, they profit. Incentives misaligned by design. Withdrawal issues common with less regulated operators.
Leverage up to 1:1000 is commonplace. OTC derivatives — not exchange-cleared or exchange-regulated. A gray area many retail traders don't fully understand when they sign up.
This is the dominant model not because it serves traders well — but because it is profitable for brokers.
Problem 2 — The cash and exchange-traded gap is almost entirely unfilled
For traders who want regulated, exchange-traded, transparent products on mobile — there is almost no one
IBKR
Desktop-first, complex, built for institutional-leaning users. Not designed for the 25–35 UAE demographic.
The only mobile-first cash broker
One meaningful mobile-first cash broker in this space. Limited charting, no order depth or DOM during execution, steep options pricing. One player — that is a gap, not a market.
What the underserved trader actually wants
Mobile-first. Clean UX. Real charts, order depth, DOM. Transparent pricing. Affordable commissions. Exchange-traded. Built for someone done with CFD brokers but not ready for IBKR. This is Wio V2.
Problem 3 — Onboarding friction is limiting top-of-funnel growth
A banking-first flow creates friction for investment-only users
Wio's current model requires opening a bank account to invest. Central bank regulation means heavy KYC, multiple questionnaires, no UAE Pass integration. Not everyone who wants to invest wants or needs a Wio bank account — many already bank elsewhere.
The result: a signup process that can take significantly longer than it needs to. In 2026, leading investment platforms integrate UAE Pass — KYC is largely solved, and signup can be completed in minutes. This is increasingly table stakes.
A potential approach worth considering
Wio Invest as a separate onboarding track within the existing app — similar to how Wio Savings is a distinct product. UAE Pass integration for investments-only signup. No bank account required upfront. Regulatory questionnaires are unavoidable but could be sequenced better — don't front-load everything before the user sees any value.
The bank account relationship could be introduced later in the journey — once the user is invested, engaged, and trusts the platform. A natural cross-sell rather than a prerequisite.
This observation is based on personal experience going through the signup process — not derived from the dataset. The dataset shows strong activation within onboarded users; the opportunity is growing the top of that funnel.

Cost considerations — qualitative
Detailed cost modelling requires internal engineering, compliance, and operational input not available externally. The following buckets are indicative of what a launch of this nature would involve:
Tech build & integration
Options chain UI, DOM, TradingView integration, UAE Pass integration. One-time capital expenditure.
Regulatory & compliance
Partner agreements, legal review, regulatory filing and liaison. One-time.
Marketing & activation
Targeted to existing power and active trader segments. Low CAC — the audience already exists on platform.
Ongoing operational
Support staff training for derivatives, market data fees, clearing partner costs. Annual recurring.
Year 1 will carry one-time setup costs across tech, regulatory, and compliance — dampening net contribution. However, given Wio's existing user base (likely materially larger than the 35,605 traders in the 2025 dataset), early adoption could offset a significant portion of these costs. Profitability in Year 1 is plausible in base and upside scenarios. Year 2 onwards the one-time costs are fully absorbed and revenue compounds.
Dataset caveat
All analysis is based on the dataset provided — 35,605 traders over a 5-month window (Apr–Aug 2025). Wio's current user base is likely materially larger given platform growth since then. All projections, revenue estimates, and adoption assumptions should be read as conservative floors — the same assumptions applied to current user numbers would compound these figures meaningfully.
